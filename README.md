Rocket Vertical Hover Control Simulation (PD-Controlled) 1. Purpose Reusable launch vehicles and planetary landers require precise vertical control under gravity, uncertainty, and actuator limits. This project implements a one-dimensional rocket hover simulation to study how classical feedback control stabilizes vertical motion and how real-world non-idealities affect performance. The focus is on correctness, robustness, and interpretability rather than visual realism. 2. System Scope and Assumptions The vehicle is modeled as a point mass moving in one vertical axis. Mass and gravity are constant, and aerodynamic forces and fuel consumption are intentionally omitted. These assumptions isolate the guidance and control problem while preserving the dominant dynamics relevant to powered ascent and hover. 3. Non-Idealities and Constraints To reflect operational conditions, velocity measurements are corrupted with Gaussian noise, thrust commands are limited by a maximum actuator capability, and ground contact is enforced to prevent nonphysical states. These constraints expose realistic failure modes and highlight the interaction between control laws and hardware limits. 9. Observed Behavior With critically damped gains, the vehicle ascends smoothly to the commanded altitude and settles without sustained oscillation. Sensor noise introduces small steady-state fluctuations, while thrust saturation demonstrates how limited authority affects transient response. The system remains stable across extended simulation time. 10. Extensions This framework can be extended to include aerodynamic drag, time-varying mass, planar or three-dimensional motion, and more advanced control strategies such as LQR or MPC. State estimation techniques such as Kalman filtering would further improve robustness in noisy environments.
